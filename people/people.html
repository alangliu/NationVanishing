Vanishing Stories

As rising tides wash away lands, island culture and way of life are being threatened too. These are the stories of the pacific islanders as told in their own words.

UN Climate Summit
Kathy Jetnil-Kijiner
A Poem to my Daughter

Marshallese poet Kathy Jetnil-Kijiner spoke on behalf of civil society during the opening ceremony of the UN Climate Summit in New York City.

She performed her poem entitled "Dear Matafele Peinem" written to her daughter.

North Tarawa, Kiribati
Lisa Rawekai
Salty Water

”My name is Lisa Rawekai. I am 18 years old and from Buota, a small village in North Tarawa.  Since I was a little girl, I used this well to get fresh water from it. Recently, the water has been getting salty because of climate change. We don't have fresh water from home anymore, so I have to walk to the next village to get it for my family.”

Auckland, New Zealand
Reverend Suamalie
Our Identity

Rev. Suamalie NT Iosefa is the leader of a Tuvaluan church in New Zealand. The migrant Tuvaluan community has purchased 10 acres of land on the outskirts of Auckland to be turned into a retirement village and nursery.

Suamalie hopes to preserve the Tuvaluan language, culture, and identity for future generations, whether or not Tuvalu continues to exist.

Tarawa, Kiribati
TANGAROA AROBATI
Ancestors

Tangaroa Arobati, a high school teacher from Kiribati, recounts floating coffins as they were unearthed from the flooding of a cemetery.

Locals often are forced to move their relative’s remains and graves further inland to save them from incoming tides.
